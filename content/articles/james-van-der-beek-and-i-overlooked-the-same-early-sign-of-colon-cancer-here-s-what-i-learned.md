## Introduction
Colon cancer, also known as colorectal cancer, is a type of cancer that affects the colon or rectum. It is one of the most common types of cancer in the United States, with over 150,000 new cases diagnosed each year. Recently, there has been a rise in colon cancer cases among young people, with many overlooking early signs and symptoms. The death of **James Van Der Beek** has raised awareness about the importance of colon cancer screening and early detection. In this article, we will discuss the early signs and symptoms of colon cancer, the importance of screening, and what you can do to reduce your risk.

## Background & Context
Colon cancer is a type of cancer that occurs when abnormal cells in the colon or rectum grow and multiply uncontrollably. It is often associated with older adults, but there has been a significant increase in cases among young people in recent years. According to the **American Cancer Society**, the incidence of colon cancer among people under 50 has increased by 22% since 2010. This trend has been linked to a variety of factors, including changes in diet, lifestyle, and genetics. **NBC News** reports that doctors are now recommending that people start getting colonoscopies at a younger age, especially if they have a family history of colon cancer.

### Risk Factors
There are several risk factors that can increase your chances of developing colon cancer. These include:
* Family history of colon cancer
* Personal history of colon polyps or inflammatory bowel disease
* Low-fiber diet
* High-fat diet
* Physical inactivity
* Obesity
* Smoking

## Analysis
The rise in colon cancer cases among young people is a concerning trend that highlights the importance of early detection and screening. **KING5.com** reports that more young people are being diagnosed with colorectal cancer, with many cases going undetected until it's too late. The good news is that colon cancer is highly treatable if caught early. Regular colonoscopies can detect polyps and other abnormalities before they become cancerous, and early treatment can significantly improve survival rates.
