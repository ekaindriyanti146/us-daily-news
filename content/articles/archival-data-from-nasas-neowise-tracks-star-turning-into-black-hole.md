## Introduction
NASA's **NEOWISE** mission has made a groundbreaking discovery, tracking a star as it transforms into a **black hole**. The archival data, collected by the **NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL)**, provides a unique glimpse into the life cycle of stars and their eventual collapse. This phenomenon is a rare and significant event, offering scientists a chance to study the formation of **black holes** and the role they play in the universe. The discovery was made possible by the **NEOWISE** mission, which has been detecting and characterizing near-Earth objects and other celestial phenomena since its launch.

## Background & Context
The **NEOWISE** mission is an extension of the **WISE** (Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer) mission, which was launched in 2009. The **WISE** mission was designed to survey the entire sky in infrared light, detecting and characterizing a wide range of celestial objects, including **asteroids**, **comets**, and **near-Earth objects**. The **NEOWISE** mission, which began in 2013, has continued this work, using the **WISE** spacecraft to detect and track near-Earth objects and other celestial phenomena. The mission has been highly successful, discovering thousands of near-Earth objects and providing valuable insights into the formation and evolution of our solar system.

### The Science Behind Black Hole Formation
The formation of a **black hole** is a complex and fascinating process. It begins with the collapse of a massive star, which can occur when the star runs out of fuel and can no longer support its own weight. As the star collapses, its density increases, and its gravity becomes so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape. This point of no return is called the **event horizon**, and it marks the boundary of the **black hole**. The **NEOWISE** mission has provided scientists with a unique opportunity to study this process, tracking the transformation of a star into a **black hole** and gaining valuable insights into the life cycle of stars and the formation of **black holes**.
