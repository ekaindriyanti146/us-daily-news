## Background & Context
Polar bears (Ursus maritimus) are well adapted to the Arctic environment, with a thick layer of fat and a white coat that helps them blend in with the snow and ice. However, the rapid warming of the Arctic is altering their habitat and making it difficult for them to survive. The sea ice, which is essential for polar bears' hunting and breeding habits, is melting at an alarming rate. This has forced polar bears to spend more time on land, where they have limited access to food and are more vulnerable to human-polar bear conflicts.

The photo of the polar bear mom and her cubs resting in mud is a stark reminder of the challenges faced by these animals in a warming world. The mud, which is a result of the melting permafrost, provides a cooler surface for the bears to rest on, but it is also a sign of the degradation of their habitat. The loss of sea ice and the degradation of their habitat are not the only threats faced by polar bears. They are also vulnerable to other climate-related impacts, such as changes in the distribution and abundance of their prey, and increased human-polar bear conflicts.

## Analysis
