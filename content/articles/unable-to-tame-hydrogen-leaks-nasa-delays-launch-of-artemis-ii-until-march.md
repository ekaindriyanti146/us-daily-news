## Introduction
The National Aeronautics and Space Administration (**NASA**) has announced a delay in the launch of the **Artemis II** mission, citing issues with hydrogen leaks during a rehearsal of the mission. The new launch date is set for March, a month later than initially planned. The **Artemis II** mission is a crucial step towards **NASA**'s goal of returning humans to the moon by 2025. The mission will send astronauts on a lunar flyby, testing the capabilities of the **Space Launch System (SLS)** rocket and the **Orion** spacecraft.

## Background & Context
The **Artemis II** mission is part of **NASA**'s **Artemis** program, which aims to establish a sustainable human presence on the moon. The program is a follow-up to the **Apollo** program, which successfully landed humans on the moon in the late 1960s and early 1970s. The **Artemis** program is a more ambitious effort, with the goal of establishing a permanent human presence on the moon and eventually sending humans to Mars.

The **Artemis II** mission will be the first crewed mission of the **Artemis** program, with four astronauts on board the **Orion** spacecraft. The mission will test the capabilities of the **SLS** rocket and the **Orion** spacecraft, as well as the astronauts' ability to survive in space for an extended period.

The delay in the launch of the **Artemis II** mission is a result of issues during a rehearsal of the mission, which was conducted to test the systems and procedures of the mission. The rehearsal revealed issues with hydrogen leaks, which **NASA** is working to resolve.

### Hydrogen Leaks: A Critical Issue
Hydrogen leaks are a critical issue in space exploration, as they can pose a significant risk to the safety of the astronauts and the success of the mission. Hydrogen is a highly flammable gas, and leaks can lead to explosions or fires. **NASA** is working to resolve the issue, but it is a complex problem that requires careful attention to detail.
