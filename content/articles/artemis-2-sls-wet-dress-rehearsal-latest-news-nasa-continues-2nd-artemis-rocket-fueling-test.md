## Introduction
The **National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA)** is currently conducting a second wet dress rehearsal for its **Artemis 2 mission**, a crucial step towards returning humans to the lunar surface by 2025. The mission will send the first woman and the first person of color to the lunar surface, marking a significant milestone in the history of space exploration. The **Space Launch System (SLS) rocket**, which will be used for the mission, is undergoing a series of tests to ensure its safety and reliability. In this article, we will delve into the details of the **Artemis 2 mission**, its objectives, and the challenges that **NASA** is facing in its pursuit of lunar exploration.

## Background & Context
The **Artemis program** was launched in 2019, with the goal of returning humans to the lunar surface by 2024. However, due to various delays and technical issues, the mission has been pushed back to 2025. The **Artemis 2 mission** is a critical step towards achieving this goal, as it will test the **Orion spacecraft** and its crew capsule, as well as the **SLS rocket**. The mission will also test the **European Service Module**, which will provide propulsion, life support, and communication systems for the **Orion spacecraft**. The **Artemis 2 mission** will be an uncrewed mission, but it will pave the way for future crewed missions to the lunar surface.

The **SLS rocket** is a heavy-lift rocket that will be used for the **Artemis 2 mission**. It is designed to carry the **Orion spacecraft** and its crew capsule to the lunar surface. The rocket is powered by four **RS-25 engines**, which are the same engines used in the **Space Shuttle program**. The **SLS rocket** is also equipped with two **solid rocket boosters**, which will provide additional thrust during liftoff.
