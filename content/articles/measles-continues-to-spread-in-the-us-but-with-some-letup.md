## Introduction
Measles, a highly contagious and potentially deadly disease, continues to spread in the US, with a notable increase in cases in South Carolina. According to recent reports, the number of measles cases in the state has risen to 876, with 29 new cases reported in the past week alone. This outbreak has raised concerns about the effectiveness of vaccination efforts and the potential for further spread of the disease. In this article, we will examine the current state of the measles outbreak in the US, the causes and consequences of the disease, and the efforts being made to contain it.

## Background & Context
Measles is a viral disease that spreads through the air when an infected person coughs or sneezes. It can cause a range of symptoms, including fever, cough, runny nose, and a characteristic rash. In severe cases, measles can lead to complications such as brain swelling, pneumonia, and death. The disease is highly contagious, with a single infected person able to spread it to up to 18 others. The US had previously been declared measles-free in 2000, but the disease has made a comeback in recent years, with outbreaks reported in several states.

The current outbreak in South Carolina is particularly concerning, as it has led to a significant increase in cases and has raised fears about the potential for further spread. Health officials are working to contain the outbreak, but the fact that measles is highly contagious and can spread quickly makes it a challenging task. The outbreak has also highlighted the importance of vaccination in preventing the spread of the disease. The **[measles vaccine](https://www.cdc.gov/vaccines/vpd/measles/index.html)** is highly effective in preventing measles, but some people may not be vaccinated due to medical reasons or personal beliefs.
