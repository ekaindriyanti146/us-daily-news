## Background & Context
Bredt's rule, named after the German chemist **Julius Bredt**, was first proposed in the early 20th century. It states that certain types of molecules, known as "bridged alkenes," cannot exist due to their unstable structure. These molecules have a specific arrangement of atoms, which creates a "bridge" between two carbon atoms. According to Bredt's rule, this bridge is too strained, making it impossible for the molecule to form. However, the recent discovery has shown that it is possible to create these molecules, albeit with some modifications.

### The Discovery
The researchers, led by a team of scientists from a prestigious university, used advanced computational methods and experimental techniques to create the new molecules. They designed a series of "hyperpyramidalized alkenes" with bond orders near 1.5, which are significantly different from traditional alkenes. These molecules have a unique structure, with a "cage-like" arrangement of atoms, which allows them to exist in a stable state. The team used a combination of theoretical calculations and experimental methods, including nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy and X-ray crystallography, to characterize the new molecules.
