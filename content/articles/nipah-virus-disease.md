## Introduction
The **Nipah virus** outbreak in India has raised alarm bells globally, with the **World Health Organization (WHO)** closely monitoring the situation. The virus, which is zoonotic, meaning it can be transmitted between animals and humans, has been detected in several states in India, including Kerala and Tamil Nadu. As the situation continues to unfold, it is essential to understand the key facts about the Nipah virus and its implications for global health.

The Nipah virus is a type of **henipavirus**, which is typically found in bats and other animals. The virus can be transmitted to humans through contact with infected animals, such as bats, pigs, or horses, or through contaminated food and water. Symptoms of the Nipah virus include fever, headache, and respiratory problems, which can progress to more severe conditions, such as encephalitis and pneumonia.

## Background & Context
The Nipah virus was first identified in 1998 in Malaysia and Singapore, where it caused a significant outbreak. Since then, there have been several outbreaks of the virus in different parts of the world, including India, Bangladesh, and Cambodia. The virus is considered a significant public health concern due to its high mortality rate, which can range from 40% to 90% depending on the severity of the outbreak.

The current outbreak in India has been linked to the consumption of date palm sap, which is a popular beverage in the region. The Indian government has taken measures to contain the outbreak, including setting up isolation wards and conducting contact tracing.
