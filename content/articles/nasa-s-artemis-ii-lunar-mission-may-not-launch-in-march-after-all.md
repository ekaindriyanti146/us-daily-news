## Introduction
The National Aeronautics and Space Administration (**NASA**) has announced that its **Artemis II** lunar mission may not launch in March as previously planned. The mission, which aims to send four astronauts on a journey around the Moon, has been delayed due to technical issues with the rocket's upper stage. This setback is a significant blow to NASA's plans to return humans to the Moon by 2025. In this article, we will delve into the background and context of the mission, analyze the implications of the delay, and explore what's next for the **Artemis II** program.

## Background & Context
The **Artemis II** mission is a critical step towards establishing a sustainable human presence on the Moon. The mission will test the capabilities of the **Space Launch System (SLS)** rocket and the **Orion** spacecraft, which will be used for future lunar missions. The four astronauts on board will spend approximately 26 days in space, including 10 days in lunar orbit, conducting scientific experiments and testing the systems and procedures necessary for a manned mission to the Moon. The mission is also an important milestone in NASA's plans to send humans to Mars in the 2030s.

The **Artemis II** mission has been in development for several years, with NASA working closely with its partners, including **Lockheed Martin**, **Boeing**, and **Northrop Grumman**, to design and build the necessary hardware. The mission has already faced several delays and setbacks, including a delay in the development of the **SLS** rocket and issues with the **Orion** spacecraft's heat shield. Despite these challenges, NASA has made significant progress in recent months, with the successful completion of several critical tests, including a [**wet dress rehearsal**](https://www.nasa.gov/artemis/program/wet-dress-rehearsal) of the **SLS** rocket.
