## Background & Context
Dark matter is a mysterious substance that is thought to make up approximately 27% of the universe. Despite its abundance, dark matter is incredibly difficult to detect, as it does not emit or reflect any light, making it invisible to our telescopes. However, its presence can be inferred by its gravitational effects on visible matter. For example, the rotation curves of galaxies are flat, indicating that stars and gas in the outer regions of the galaxy are moving at a constant velocity, suggesting that there is a large amount of unseen mass. Similarly, the distribution of galaxy clusters and the large-scale structure of the universe can be explained by the presence of dark matter.

'Ghost particles', also known as neutrinos, are high-energy particles that are produced by the sun, stars, and other astrophysical sources. They are called 'ghost particles' because they can pass through matter without interacting with it, making them extremely difficult to detect. However, scientists have developed sophisticated detectors that can capture these particles and study their properties.
