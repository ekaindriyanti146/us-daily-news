## Introduction
Jupiter, the largest planet in our solar system, has been a subject of fascination for astronomers and scientists for centuries. Recently, new measurements have revealed that Jupiter is smaller than previously thought, with a size that is approximately 4,000 miles smaller in diameter than what was previously estimated. This discovery was made possible by **NASA's Juno Mission**, which has been studying Jupiter since 2016. In this article, we will delve into the details of this discovery, its implications, and what it means for our understanding of the planet.

## Background & Context
The **Juno Mission** was launched in 2011 with the primary goal of studying Jupiter's atmosphere, magnetic field, and interior. The spacecraft has been orbiting Jupiter since 2016, providing a wealth of new data and insights into the planet's composition and behavior. One of the key objectives of the mission was to determine Jupiter's size and shape with greater accuracy. To achieve this, the spacecraft used a combination of **gravimetry** and **atmospheric measurements** to map the planet's gravitational field and atmospheric properties.

The new measurements indicate that Jupiter's diameter is approximately 88,846 miles (142,984 kilometers), which is about 4,000 miles (6,400 kilometers) smaller than the previously estimated diameter of 92,843 miles (149,456 kilometers). This reduction in size may seem small, but it has significant implications for our understanding of the planet's internal structure and composition.
