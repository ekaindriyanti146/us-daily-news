## Introduction
The **National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA)** has officially classified the **Boeing Starliner** failure as a maximum-level Type A mishap, following an investigation into the incident. The failure occurred during an uncrewed flight test in December 2019, when the spacecraft failed to reach the correct orbit due to a software issue. The incident has raised concerns about the safety and reliability of the Starliner spacecraft, which is intended to carry astronauts to the **International Space Station**. In this article, we will examine the causes of the failure, the response from NASA and Boeing, and the implications for the future of space travel.

## Background & Context
The Boeing Starliner is a commercial crew spacecraft developed by **Boeing** in partnership with NASA. The spacecraft is designed to carry astronauts to the International Space Station and return them safely to Earth. The uncrewed flight test in December 2019 was intended to demonstrate the spacecraft's capabilities and prepare for crewed missions. However, the spacecraft failed to reach the correct orbit due to a software issue, and the mission was aborted. An independent report has since been released, sharply criticizing NASA management and Boeing for the troubled Starliner flight. The report highlights issues with the spacecraft's software and testing procedures, and recommends improvements to ensure the safety and reliability of the spacecraft.

### Investigation Findings
The investigation into the Starliner failure has identified several causes, including:
* A software issue that caused the spacecraft to misfire its thrusters and fail to reach the correct orbit
* Inadequate testing procedures, which failed to identify the software issue before the flight
* Insufficient communication and coordination between NASA and Boeing, which contributed to the failure
