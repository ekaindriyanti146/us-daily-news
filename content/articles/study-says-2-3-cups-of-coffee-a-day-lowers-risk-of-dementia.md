A recent study has found that drinking 2-3 cups of coffee a day may lower the risk of dementia. As the global population ages, the prevalence of dementia is expected to increase, making it essential to identify potential risk factors and preventive strategies. ## Background & Context
**Coffee** is one of the most widely consumed beverages globally, with billions of people drinking it every day. While excessive coffee consumption has been linked to negative health effects, such as increased heart rate and blood pressure, moderate coffee consumption has been associated with several health benefits, including improved cognitive function, reduced risk of stroke and type 2 diabetes, and lower risk of certain types of cancer. The current study adds to the growing body of evidence suggesting that moderate coffee consumption may also have neuroprotective effects and reduce the risk of dementia.

The study analyzed data from thousands of participants and found that those who consumed 2-3 cups of coffee a day had a lower risk of developing dementia compared to those who consumed less or more coffee. The study also found that **caffeinated coffee** and **tea** were more effective than decaf in reducing dementia risk, suggesting that caffeine may play a role in the association. However, more research is needed to confirm the findings and understand the underlying mechanisms.

### Study Methodology
The study used a **prospective cohort design**, which involved following a large group of participants over time to examine the relationship between coffee consumption and dementia risk. The participants were asked to complete a questionnaire about their coffee consumption habits, and their cognitive function was assessed using standardized tests. The study controlled for potential confounding variables, such as age, sex, education level, and lifestyle factors, to ensure that the association between coffee consumption and dementia risk was not due to other factors.
