## Background & Context
Snakes are a diverse group of animals, with over 3,000 species found on every continent except Antarctica. They are carnivores, feeding on a wide range of prey, from small mammals to birds and other reptiles. However, in some cases, snakes have been observed eating other snakes, a behavior known as cannibalism. This phenomenon has been reported in various species, including the **garter snake**, **king snake**, and **cobra**. But what drives this behavior, and why is it becoming more common?

### Environmental Pressures
One possible explanation for the evolution of cannibalism in snakes is environmental pressure. In areas where food is scarce, snakes may turn to eating other snakes as a way to obtain essential nutrients and energy. This behavior may also be driven by competition for resources, such as mating opportunities or territory. For example, in areas with high snake densities, cannibalism may be a way for snakes to eliminate competitors and increase their chances of survival.

### Evolutionary Advantage
Cannibalism in snakes may also provide an evolutionary advantage. This behavior may also allow snakes to adapt to changing environmental conditions, such as shifts in prey populations or habitat destruction. For instance, in areas where prey populations are declining, snakes that eat other snakes may be more likely to survive and thrive.
