## Introduction
The Lunar New Year, also known as the Spring Festival, is one of the most significant traditional holidays in many East Asian cultures. This year, the Lunar New Year falls on February 17, 2026, and marks the beginning of the Year of the Fire Horse. The Fire Horse is the seventh animal in the 12-year cycle of the Chinese zodiac and is associated with energy, passion, and adventure. As the world welcomes the new year, celebrations are taking place in many countries, with people coming together to mark the occasion with parades, fireworks, and traditional foods.

## Background & Context
The Lunar New Year has a rich history that dates back over 3,000 years. It is a time for family reunions, gift-giving, and reflection on the past year. The holiday is also associated with many traditions and customs, such as the giving of red envelopes filled with money, the eating of traditional foods like dumplings and niangao, and the display of couplets and lanterns. In China, the Lunar New Year is a week-long holiday, during which many businesses and schools are closed. The holiday is also celebrated in other countries with significant Chinese populations, such as Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Singapore.

### History of the Lunar New Year
The Lunar New Year has its roots in ancient Chinese mythology. According to legend, the holiday originated during the Shang Dynasty, when people would sacrifice to the gods and ancestors at the beginning of the new year. Over time, the holiday evolved and became associated with the Chinese zodiac, a cycle of 12 animals that represents the different years. The Fire Horse is one of the most dynamic and energetic animals in the zodiac, and its presence is expected to bring excitement and adventure to the new year.
