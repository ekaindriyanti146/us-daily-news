## Introduction
On January 28, 1986, the Space Shuttle Challenger exploded just 73 seconds after launching from **Kennedy Space Center**, killing all seven crew members on board. The accident was a devastating blow to the nation and the space program, and it led to a significant overhaul of NASA's safety procedures and culture. Today, 40 years after the accident, NASA and the families of the fallen astronauts are still remembering and honoring the crew members who lost their lives in the pursuit of space exploration.

## Background & Context
The Space Shuttle Challenger was launched on a cold winter morning, with temperatures well below freezing. The crew members on board were **Francis "Dick" Scobee**, **Michael J. Smith**, **Judith A. Resnik**, **Ellison Onizuka**, **Ronald E. McNair**, **Sharon Christa McAuliffe**, and **Gregory Jarvis**. The mission was intended to be a routine one, with the crew members conducting experiments and deploying satellites. However, just 73 seconds after launch, the solid rocket booster failed, causing a catastrophic explosion that destroyed the shuttle.

The investigation into the accident revealed that a faulty O-ring in one of the solid rocket boosters had failed due to the low temperatures. The O-ring was designed to seal the joint between the booster and the fuel tank, but it failed to do so, allowing hot gases to escape and ignite the fuel tank. The accident was a wake-up call for NASA, highlighting the importance of safety procedures and the need for a culture of safety within the organization.
